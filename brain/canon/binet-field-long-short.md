# Les Binet + Peter Field — The Long and the Short of It

> *"Brand advertising drives long-term growth. Activation advertising drives short-term sales. Neither alone is sufficient. The ratio that works is 60:40 brand-to-activation for most consumer businesses — and the evidence is overwhelming."*

The empirical evidence layer that underpins Byron Sharp's theoretical claims. Binet + Field analyzed 10+ years of IPA Effectiveness Awards data — the largest dataset of ad effectiveness studies ever assembled — and produced the 60:40 rule and the brand-vs-activation dichotomy that every modern media planner still references.

Where Sharp tells you *reach matters*, Binet + Field tell you *how much to spend on what, and why*.

---

## The Central Finding: 60:40

Across thousands of campaigns analyzed:
- **Brand-building advertising** (long-term, emotional, broad reach) drove the majority of revenue growth.
- **Activation / sales-promotion advertising** (short-term, rational, targeted) drove the minority — but was still essential.
- **Optimum mix: ~60% brand, ~40% activation.**
- **Below 60% brand = short-term-profit bias = long-term decline.**
- **Above 60% brand = under-activation = leaves revenue on table.**

Different verticals adjust: B2B closer to 50:50 or even 55:45 activation-leaning. But the principle holds — you need BOTH, and brand is usually under-invested.

---

## Long vs. Short — What They Actually Mean

| | Brand (Long) | Activation (Short) |
|---|---|---|
| Time horizon | 6-24+ months | 0-30 days |
| Target | Broad — light buyers + future buyers | Narrow — in-market buyers |
| Message | Emotional, memorable, distinctive | Rational, specific, urgent |
| KPI | Mental availability, brand metrics, unaided recall | Click, lead, sale, conversion |
| Creative | Story, character, fame | Offer, deadline, CTA |
| Common examples | TV, OOH, long video, podcast sponsorships | Performance ads, PPC, retargeting, promo emails |

**The trap:** activation is measurable; brand is hard to measure. So finance defaults to activation, cuts brand, and growth quietly decays over 18-36 months.

## Binet + Field's Other Key Findings

### Emotional campaigns outperform rational ones
Rational campaigns beat emotional ones on *short-term* KPIs. But emotional campaigns win on *long-term* growth by a large margin. The data is unambiguous.

### Fame is the biggest single predictor of effectiveness
Not awareness. FAME — the sense that "everyone seems to be talking about this brand." This is Sharp's mental availability operationalized.

### Reach beats frequency (at brand stage)
Mirrors Sharp. Activation flips this — narrow frequency beats broad reach for in-market capture.

### B2B needs brand too — often more than it thinks
The common B2B excuse ("our audience is too small for brand") is wrong at every market size they studied. Even in small B2B niches, brand-building outperforms pure activation over 12+ months.

### Creative quality compounds
Good creative doesn't just convert better in the short run — it also builds brand faster. Bad creative does the opposite, on BOTH axes.

## The 60:40 Test — Apply to Any Brand

For each brand Maven runs, answer:

1. **What % of marketing spend is brand-building vs. activation?**
2. **What % of content output is brand-building (educational, emotional, fame) vs. activation (offer-led, promo, ad)?**
3. **What % of creative hours go to brand vs. activation?**

If any of these is below 50% brand: under-investing. Diagnose why.

For OASIS at sub-$50K MRR: realistic target is 55:45 brand-to-activation, rising to 60:40 as brand compounds. Activation-heavy phase early is acceptable if brand work is happening — and CC's personal content counts as brand (see [[conaugh-personal]]).
