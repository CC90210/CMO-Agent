# Sean Ellis — The PMF Engine

> *"The only way to grow predictably is to have product-market fit. And the only way to know if you have it is to ask the users the right question."*

Sean Ellis — ex-Dropbox growth, coined the term "growth hacking" — built the canonical test for product-market fit: **"How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"** If 40%+ of users answer "very disappointed," the product has PMF. Below 40%, it doesn't — regardless of what the founders think.

Simple. Cheap. Still the gold standard.

---

## The Ellis PMF Survey

Five questions, usually emailed to active users within 30 days of signup:

1. **How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?**
   - a) Very disappointed
   - b) Somewhat disappointed
   - c) Not disappointed
   - d) N/A — I no longer use [product]
2. **What type of people do you think would most benefit from [product]?**
3. **What is the main benefit you receive from [product]?**
4. **How can we improve [product] for you?**
5. **Have you recommended [product] to anyone?**

**The magic number:** ≥40% answering "Very disappointed" = PMF achieved. Below 40% = keep iterating.

Ellis's research across 100+ startups: 40% was the inflection point at which growth became mechanical rather than effortful.

---

## What the Survey Actually Tells You

### Question 1 (the 40% threshold)
Headline PMF metric. But note: it's a *lagging* indicator on the journey. Use it as the gate before investing in growth channels.

### Question 2 (who'd benefit)
Your users' description of your ICP — in their words — is often more useful than your own ICP document. This is the raw material for positioning copy.

### Question 3 (main benefit)
What value users ACTUALLY get (vs. the value you THINK you're delivering). Often surprising. Often should rewrite the sales page entirely.

### Question 4 (improvement)
Churn signals hide here. Patterns across answers = product roadmap.

### Question 5 (recommendation)
Net Promoter proxy + organic growth coefficient. Low recommendation rate is a reach-rate ceiling regardless of paid acquisition.

## Pre-PMF vs. Post-PMF Marketing

Ellis's controversial claim: **pre-PMF marketing is mostly wasted.** You can't scale growth for a product people don't yet sufficiently love. The right pre-PMF work is:
- Talking to users (see [[fitzpatrick-mom-test]])
- Iterating product
- Measuring PMF signal monthly

Paid ads pre-PMF = burning money. Content engine pre-PMF = OK because it builds an audience for post-PMF launch. Cold outreach pre-PMF = burning your list.

**Post-PMF**, the playbook flips:
- Pick 1-2 growth channels that fit your Model-Channel Fit (see [[balfour-4-fits]])
- Scale them relentlessly
- Layer on referral + retention loops
- Experiment aggressively

## The Ellis Must-Have Score (Advanced)

For products that survey at 30-39% (threshold-borderline):
- Segment users by the question 3 answer. Often ONE segment is at 60%+ disappointed while others are at 15%.
- That 60% segment is your PMF beachhead.
- Redirect marketing + product toward that segment only.

This is how Superhuman got to PMF — segmented, found the 60% cluster, redirected everything.

## How Maven Applies This

- **Every brand runs the Ellis survey at 90-day intervals.**
  - OASIS: first survey after 10+ clients on retainer.
  - PropFlow: first survey after 20+ beta users.
  - Nostalgic: survey current DJ userbase to find the 40% cluster (if any).
- **Pre-PMF paid ad budget = $0 default.** Exceptions require a specific test hypothesis and kill criteria. (This is coupled with the Atlas spend-gate — see [[PATTERNS]].)
- **Post-survey action:** question 3 answers (the "main benefit" column) rewrites the sales page hero within 30 days.
- **Question 4 (improvement) answers feed back to Bravo for product roadmap.** Maven ≠ product, but Maven surfaces the signal.
- **Segmented PMF for borderline scores.** If aggregate is 30-39%, don't conclude "not PMF" — segment and find the cluster.

## Anti-Patterns This Canon Catches

- **"We have PMF because our MRR is growing."** MRR growth without PMF signal = growth from paid acquisition that'll stall. Ellis survey is the lagging confirmation.
- **"We're focused on growth before we have PMF."** Growth without PMF burns money. Ellis's rule: PMF FIRST.
- **"Let's run a big paid ads campaign to validate interest."** Paid ads validate ad-creative, not PMF. Only users who stick around signal PMF.
- **"NPS is enough."** NPS measures willingness to recommend. PMF measures willingness to lose the product. Different questions, different implications.
