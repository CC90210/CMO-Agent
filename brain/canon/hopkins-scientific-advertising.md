# Claude Hopkins — Scientific Advertising

> *"Advertising has been, until recently, a gamble... We are coming now to science and certainty. Sales are traced, causes are studied. Successful ads are run in thousands of publications. Each one reveals its own results. We learn just what appeals convert."*

The grandfather of A/B testing, tracked coupons, and measurable advertising. Written in 1923. Every lesson in *Scientific Advertising* still holds because human psychology hasn't changed. Ogilvy claimed no one should enter advertising without reading this book seven times.

---

## The Iron Principles (still true in 2026)

### 1. Advertising is salesmanship in print
Every ad is a salesperson. The copy answers the questions the prospect would ask a salesperson in a store. No cleverness for its own sake — cleverness distracts from the sale.

### 2. Specifics beat generalities — always
- "Cuts cleaning time in half" loses to "Cuts cleaning time from 40 minutes to 20."
- "Saves you money" loses to "$37/month average savings across 4,200 tracked customers."
- **Claim specificity IS proof.** Vague claims signal lack of evidence.

### 3. Offer a reason-why
Don't say "It's the best." Say *why* it's the best — and make the why unique to your product, even if the characteristic is industry-standard. (Hopkins famously made Schlitz beer famous by describing how they sterilized bottles in live steam — every brewery did this, but no one had ever *said* it.)

### 4. Test everything
Every claim, every headline, every offer. Keyed coupons, split tests, A/B response rates. "The only purpose of advertising is to make sales. It is profitable or unprofitable according to its actual sales."

### 5. Track to the penny
What doesn't get measured gets guessed. Hopkins kept spreadsheets of response rates per publication, per headline, per offer, in 1915.

### 6. Free samples + trial offers beat claims
Remove risk. The prospect who tries buys at 3-10x the rate of the prospect who doesn't.

### 7. Write to individuals, not crowds
Every ad reads as if written to ONE person. "You" language. "Dear Reader" register. Never "our customers" or "people like you." One person, one letter, one pitch.

### 8. Use names, faces, and stories — not stock
Testimonial with name + photo + specific quote > generic "our customers love us." Hopkins insisted on real people in every ad he wrote.

### 9. Long copy outsells short copy when the traffic is cold
Contrary to "people don't read long copy" — Hopkins's tests showed longer, detail-rich copy converted better for considered purchases and cold audiences. (Re-validated 100 years later in every direct-response shop on Earth.)

### 10. The more you TELL, the more you SELL
Every feature explained. Every objection handled in the copy before the prospect asks. The ad is the salesperson — give it all the ammunition.

## What Hopkins Catches That Modern Marketing Misses

- **"Brand advertising doesn't need to be measured."** — Hopkins rejected this in 1923. Every ad budget is measurable if you set it up right.
- **"Clever wins."** — Hopkins's axiom: clever distracts. Clear beats clever on every response test he ever ran.
- **"Short copy is what people want."** — Short copy wins when the audience already knows you. For cold traffic, short copy = invisible.
- **"Images over words."** — Hopkins used images only when they served the argument. Decoration without argument = wasted ink.

## How Maven Applies This

- **Every claim in Maven's copy is specific.** Replace "many" with a number. Replace "fast" with a duration. Replace "better" with a benchmark.
- **Every campaign has keyed tracking.** UTMs on every link. Ad-level content_id registered in [[content_registry]]. [[ATTRIBUTION_MODEL]] is the 2026 version of Hopkins's keyed coupons.
- **Every testimonial includes first name + face + specific metric.** Anonymous "5-star" signals fakery. "Jordan, HVAC owner, Ontario — booked 11 calls in 30 days" signals truth.
- **Every cold-traffic ad tests long vs. short** before concluding short "works better." Usually long wins on cold.
- **Every "we're the best" claim gets a reason-why attached** or gets deleted.

## The Hopkins Test (for any draft)

Before shipping a piece of copy, ask:
1. **Is every claim specific?** (numbers, names, benchmarks)
2. **Is there a reason-why attached to every superlative?**
3. **Does it read as written to one person?**
4. **Is there a risk-removal component?** (guarantee, trial, sample)
5. **Can I track response to this specific piece?** (keyed / UTM'd)

Miss any one = iterate.
