# Geoffrey Moore — Crossing the Chasm

> *"Most technology companies fail not because of a bad product, but because they can't cross the chasm between early-adopters and the pragmatist mainstream."*

The canonical framework for how technology products actually get adopted. Moore took Everett Rogers's innovation-adoption curve and identified the chasm — the gap between early enthusiasts and pragmatist buyers — that kills most tech startups silently.

This is the foundational SaaS-marketing text. Applies directly to **PropFlow** (pre-chasm), and to OASIS's "AI automation" category which is early-majority-crossing *right now*.

---

## The Adoption Curve

```
Innovators (2.5%) → Early Adopters (13.5%) → [THE CHASM] → Early Majority (34%) → Late Majority (34%) → Laggards (16%)
```

| Segment | Buying motivation | Evidence they need |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Innovators** | New tech for its own sake | "Does it work?" |
| **Early adopters** | Strategic advantage, competitive edge | Visionary fit, upside story |
| **Early majority** | Productivity, status quo improvement | Proven references in my exact segment |
| **Late majority** | Don't-fall-behind, compliance | Industry-standard, safe, de-risked |
| **Laggards** | Forced adoption | Vendor pressure, no alternative |

The Chasm sits between early adopters and early majority. Why?
- Early adopters buy VISION. They're willing to tolerate roughness for strategic upside.
- Early majority buys PROOF. They need references from people just like them. They will NOT buy based on vision.
- Most startups have early adopters AND pitch visionary messaging to early majority — and fail.

---

## Moore's Key Insight: The Bowling Alley

The way to cross the chasm is to **dominate a beachhead** — a single vertical / use-case / niche where you become THE undisputed leader. Then use that dominance as a reference to topple the adjacent niche. Then the next. Like bowling pins.

- **Beachhead strategy for OASIS:** dominate "DM automation for owner-operator service businesses, $250K-$2M ARR, Ontario." Every piece of content, every case study, every ad speaks to that niche *only*. Once 20-50 customers in that niche, expand to adjacent (wellness → HVAC → other service verticals).
- **Beachhead strategy for PropFlow:** dominate "small PM firms in Ontario, 5-50 doors" before trying Canada-wide. Before trying US. Before trying enterprise.
- **Anti-pattern:** trying to win "all service businesses" or "all PM firms" from day one. Unfocused messaging + unprovable references = chasm death.

## The Whole Product Concept

Moore's second crucial insight: **the product you sell is never just the product.** It's the whole product — including:
- The core product itself
- Implementation / onboarding
- Integration with adjacent systems
- Training / documentation
- Ongoing support
- Community / user base
- Reference customers
- Complementary products

Early adopters will assemble their own whole product. Early majority will NOT. They expect it pre-assembled.

**Apply:** for each brand, map the full whole-product. Which pieces do we provide? Which do we assume the customer will figure out? Which of those assumptions are chasm-breakers? Patch them before the cross.

## Moore's Positioning Formula (directly applied)

Moore's book introduced a fill-in-the-blank positioning statement that later became the Geoffrey-Moore-or-HubSpot-formula:

```
For [target customer]
Who [statement of need or opportunity]
[Product name] is a [product category]
That [key benefit — compelling reason to buy].
Unlike [primary competitive alternative]
Our product [primary differentiation].
```

This is the ancestor of Dunford's positioning template. Maven uses Dunford's richer version (see [[dunford-positioning]]), but Moore's version is the minimum viable positioning artifact. Every brand Maven runs should have the Moore-formula one-liner readable in 15 seconds.

---

## Tornado Dynamics (the sequel)

Post-chasm, Moore's *Inside the Tornado* describes what happens after early majority accepts the category: explosive adoption, category leader established, laggards forced in. The playbook changes:
- Pre-chasm: beachhead + references + whole product
- In the tornado: capture market share AS FAST AS POSSIBLE. Don't customize. Don't chase niches. Ship pandemic adoption.
- Post-tornado: Main Street market. Customization returns. Per-segment tuning.

Most SaaS categories in 2026 are in Late Majority / Main Street phase. Category leadership is locked; challengers must find new categories (see [[ries-trout-positioning]] Law 2).

## How Maven Applies This

- **Every brand assessed: which side of the chasm?** OASIS = crossing. PropFlow = pre-chasm (no early adopters yet, need to sell 5-10 before chasm concerns even apply). Nostalgic = stuck in innovator segment.
- **Beachhead doctrine:** every brand's positioning picks ONE specific niche-beachhead. Ambiguous = chasm death.
- **References disproportionately valued for early-majority segments.** Case studies from the beachhead niche are the single most valuable marketing asset for crossing. Invest creative time accordingly.
- **Whole-product audit before launch.** What pieces are we missing that early majority will balk at? Don't launch until gaps are plugged (or explicitly risk-disclosed).
- **Messaging changes across the chasm.** Vision language for early adopters → proof-and-reference language for early majority. Don't send one message to both.
