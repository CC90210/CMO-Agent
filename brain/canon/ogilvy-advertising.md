# David Ogilvy — Ogilvy on Advertising

> *"The consumer isn't a moron. She is your wife. Don't insult her intelligence."*
> *"If it doesn't sell, it isn't creative."*

Ogilvy bridged Hopkins's measurement rigor with Madison Avenue brand thinking. He built Rolls-Royce, Hathaway, Dove, and Schweppes on the same principle: *research-driven positioning + disciplined craft + respect for the reader*. Ogilvy is the bridge between "scientific advertising" (measurable) and "brand advertising" (memorable). He believed they were the same discipline.

---

## Ogilvy's Core Canon

### 1. Do your homework
Before writing a word of copy, Ogilvy wrote a product fact sheet. Every feature, every competitor, every customer type, every objection. "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals." Every piece Maven writes starts with research — or it's craft.

### 2. Headlines do 80% of the work
"Five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy." If the headline doesn't work, nothing downstream matters. Famous Ogilvy headline: "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."

### 3. Specificity builds credibility
"The electric clock" — not "the interior." Hopkins's lineage. Vague = low-credibility. Specific = high-credibility.

### 4. Promise a benefit in the headline
Every headline tests against: *"Would a busy prospect stop and read this because of what's promised?"* If the answer is "no / maybe," rewrite.

### 5. Long copy converts — when the product warrants it
Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce ad: 607 words. His Guinness ad: ~3,000 words. "The more you tell, the more you sell." Cold traffic rewards detail. Commodity products can be short; considered products need length.

### 6. Brand image is an asset — invest in it
Ogilvy's most controversial contribution: brand as a long-term investment. He told clients: your advertising must be measurable AND cumulative. Each ad sells today AND adds to the brand account. Ogilvy called this "every advertisement contributes to the complex symbol which is the brand image."

### 7. Big ideas beat incremental copy
"You can't bore people into buying your product." Ogilvy's tests: campaigns built around ONE big idea (eye-patch man for Hathaway, Commander Whitehead for Schweppes) outperformed incremental campaigns by 5-10x. One defensible big idea > ten clever small ones.

### 8. Respect the reader
Don't talk down. Don't manipulate. Don't use puffery. Consumers can smell insincerity through any amount of production value. This is Ogilvy's ethical core and also his most underrated sales insight — condescension kills conversion.

## Ogilvy's Big Ideas (that still dictate modern marketing)

- **Brand equity is a measurable balance sheet.** Every ad either adds to or subtracts from it.
- **Research precedes writing.** Always.
- **Long copy is not out of style. Lazy copy is.**
- **Creativity without selling is self-indulgence.**
- **The best ads make the PRODUCT the hero, not the campaign.**

## How Maven Applies This

- **Every campaign starts with a fact sheet.** Features, alternatives, objections, buyer types, proof points. No writing begins until the sheet is filled. Output goes to `brain/research/<brand>-voc.md`.
- **Every ad has ONE big idea.** Tested against the "can a stranger remember this in 24 hours?" rule. If not, it's clever, not big.
- **Every headline promises a benefit OR names a mechanism.** Anything else is decoration. (See [[schwartz-breakthrough-advertising]] for stage-appropriate headline types.)
- **Long copy gets a fair shot.** Especially cold traffic. Defaulting to short because "nobody reads long" is Ogilvy's exact complaint.
- **Respect check:** every piece passes an anti-condescension review. No "Are you tired of struggling...?" openers. No implied stupidity.

## Ogilvy's Anti-Canon (what he was fighting)

| Ogilvy said (1963) | Still true (2026) |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| "Don't bury the lede in cleverness." | Yes — still kills more ads than any other mistake. |
| "Humor doesn't sell." | Mostly still true — humor wins memory but loses conversion. Use for brand; not for response. |
| "Creative directors should never be decision makers on strategy." | Still true — strategy first, craft second. (See [[ritson-diagnosis]].) |
| "Puffery is a liability." | Still true — modern audiences are MORE slop-allergic, not less. |
