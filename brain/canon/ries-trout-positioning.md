# Al Ries + Jack Trout — Positioning

> *"Positioning is not what you do to a product. Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect."*

The foundational work on positioning — written in 1981, still un-matched. April Dunford's modern positioning work (see [[dunford-positioning]]) is the direct descendant, but Ries + Trout's original book and its sequel *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing* remain the clearest statement of the problem: **the mind has limited slots, and the brand that owns a slot wins the category — even if it's a worse product.**

---

## The Central Insight

The mind doesn't evaluate brands. It categorizes them.

When a prospect thinks "I need an accounting software," the mind instantly surfaces 1-3 brands (QuickBooks, Xero, FreshBooks). Every other brand is invisible — not because they're bad, but because they're not in the mental slot.

**Winning the slot = winning the category.** Everything else is detail.

---

## The 22 Immutable Laws (highlights Maven lives by)

### Law 1 — The Law of Leadership
**It's better to be first than it's to be better.**
- First in a category gets the default slot. Every subsequent entrant fights uphill.
- Apply: can't be first in automation? Be first in a subcategory. OASIS isn't "AI automation" — OASIS is "DM automation for owner-operator service businesses."

### Law 2 — The Law of Category
**If you can't be first in a category, set up a new category you can be first in.**
- This is the #1 most weaponized law in marketing. Every successful challenger brand creates a new category. (7UP = "The Uncola." Miller Lite = "lite beer.")
- Apply: Maven's work on positioning always asks: "what sub-category can we be the #1 brand of?"

### Law 3 — The Law of the Mind
**It's better to be first in the mind than first in the marketplace.**
- Being FIRST technically but unknown = losing to second-mover with superior mindshare.

### Law 4 — The Law of Perception
**Marketing is not a battle of products. It's a battle of perceptions.**
- The best product routinely loses to the best-positioned product. See Betamax vs. VHS, or Google Wave vs. Slack.

### Law 6 — The Law of Exclusivity
**Two companies cannot own the same word in the mind.**
- If "fast" is owned, you can't be "fast." Pick a different slot. This is why most "better version of [leader]" strategies fail.

### Law 10 — The Law of Division
**Over time, a category will divide and become two or more categories.**
- Beer → light beer / craft beer / non-alcoholic. Software → SaaS / vertical SaaS / low-code / AI-native.
- Apply: watch for category divisions and position for the emerging sub-category before it's obvious.

### Law 14 — The Law of Attributes
**For every attribute, there is an opposite, effective attribute.**
- "Fastest" vs. "most thorough." "Cheapest" vs. "most premium." If competitor owns one, own the opposite. Don't try to out-attribute — counter-attribute.

### Law 16 — The Law of Singularity
**In each situation, only one move will produce substantial results.**
- Most marketing "experiments" diffuse effort. The real lift is almost always one specific move.

### Law 18 — The Law of Success
**Success leads to arrogance, and arrogance to failure.**
- The biggest brand threat is internal — winning teams start believing their playbook, stop studying the market, get disrupted.

### Law 22 — The Law of Resources
**Without adequate funding, an idea won't get off the ground.**
- Brutal counterbalance. Positioning is necessary but not sufficient. You need the resources to plant the flag loudly enough that the market notices.

## Ries + Trout's Positioning Process

1. **What position do you own today (in the mind of the prospect)?** Ask them, don't guess. Use language they use.
2. **What position do you want to own?** Be specific. "The #1 X for Y in Z." Vague = invisible.
3. **Who do you have to outposition?** Name the competitor(s) whose slot you're taking.
4. **Do you have the money to pull it off?** Resources test.
5. **Can you stick with it?** Positioning is compounding. Changing every 12 months = resetting the mind clock.

---

## How This Differs from (and Completes) Dunford

[[dunford-positioning]] gives you the *structured process* for modern B2B: alternatives, unique attributes, value, best-fit customers, market category.

Ries + Trout give you the *cognitive war* framing: slots, mental geography, category warfare, attribute ownership.

**Use Ries + Trout to pick the battlefield.** Use Dunford to document and communicate the position. They are complementary, not competing.

## How Maven Applies This

- **Every new brand starts with "what slot are we trying to own?"** If the slot is taken, pick a sub-slot or create a new category. (See [[dunford-positioning]] for Dunford's methodology applied to filling out the position.)
- **Positioning doesn't change more than annually.** Tactical messages change monthly; positioning is a multi-year commitment. See Law 22.
- **Competitor analysis focuses on slot occupation, not feature parity.** Who owns "fast"? Who owns "trustworthy"? Who owns "easiest onboarding"? Find an open slot or take an opposite.
- **If we can't fund the position fight, we pick a smaller category.** Rather than lose to better-funded competitors in a category we can't own.

---

## Anti-Patterns This Canon Catches

- **"Let's be a better version of [competitor]."** — Law 6. If they own the word, you can't.
- **"Let's target everyone."** — Positioning for everyone = positioned for no one (parallels Godin's Smallest Viable Audience).
- **"Let's pivot the positioning every quarter."** — Kills mind-share compounding. (See [[sharp-how-brands-grow]] on mental availability.)
- **"Our product is just better, so positioning doesn't matter."** — Law 4. The market perceives, not evaluates.
