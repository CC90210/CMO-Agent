# Agency — Objection Handlers (NEPQ-aligned)

> Agency-specific objections with NEPQ-structured responses. Use in sales calls, proposals, DMs, and email replies.

**Canon**: Jeremy Miner (NEPQ — neutral tonality, neutral language, objection-inviting reframes), Blair Enns (Win Without Pitching), April Dunford (positioning resolves most objections upstream).

---

## NEPQ core principle

Never argue. Never defend. Always ask a question that invites the prospect to reflect on their own objection.

Structure: **Neutral Acknowledge → Consequence Question → Reframe Question**.

Example of WRONG: "Our pricing reflects the quality — we're premium because..."
Example of RIGHT: "That's fair to bring up. When you think about what you've tried before, what did you find worked vs. didn't work at that price point?"

---

## The 10 agency objections (most common, ranked by frequency)

### 1. "You're too expensive"
- **What they mean (decode)**: "I don't see the value yet" OR "I can't defend this internally" OR "I'm testing if you'll discount."
- **NEVER respond**: "Our price reflects quality..." (defensive, weak)
- **DO respond**: "Fair. Can I ask — compared to what? What were you expecting it to cost?"
- **Then**: listen. They'll either name an alternative (now you can position against it) OR reveal budget (now you can scope to fit).
- **Close move**: "If we can find the right scope to fit that budget, would you move forward this week?"

### 2. "We need to think about it"
- **What they mean**: you haven't addressed the actual concern. It's always one of: risk, authority, urgency, or price.
- **NEPQ response**: "Totally — when you say think about it, what specifically are you weighing?"
- **If risk**: add a guarantee / shorter commitment / pilot
- **If authority**: offer to present to the other decision-maker
- **If urgency**: bring the consequence cost forward ("if nothing changes for 60 days, what's the cost?")
- **If price**: trade scope, don't discount

### 3. "We can do it in-house"
- **Decode**: often a face-saving move; sometimes real.
- **NEPQ response**: "Makes sense — you absolutely could. What's stopped the team from building it so far?"
- **Then**: they'll surface constraints (time, expertise, priority). Now you're not competing with an imaginary in-house team; you're competing with their actual bandwidth.
- **Reframe**: "So your team CAN, but they're spending that time on [X]. If we handle this, their time frees up for [X]. Is that worth $5K/mo?"

### 4. "Send me a proposal and I'll review it"
- **What it often means**: they want out of the call without saying no.
- **Enns + Miner move**: "I can absolutely do that. Before I spend 2 hours building it out — are there any questions you have that would make the difference between this being a 'yes' or a 'no'?"
- **If they can't name one** → it's probably a soft no. "Sounds like there might be a few details we haven't covered yet — want to stay on for 10 more minutes?"
- **If they DO name one** → address it live. The proposal can follow.

### 5. "We're going to compare a few other agencies"
- **Decode**: normal and healthy. Don't panic.
- **NEPQ response**: "Smart — I'd want to too. What are you planning to evaluate them on?"
- **Then**: set the criteria in your favor. If their criteria favor a big agency, reposition: "Makes sense for [big agency task]. For [specific thing we do], here's what the tradeoff looks like..."
- **Ask for a spot in the final round**: "Once you've shortlisted, I'd love to do a 20-min call with whichever 2-3 you like most. Fair?"

### 6. "Can you guarantee results?"
- **Dangerous territory**: if you guarantee outcomes you don't control, you're liable. If you refuse, you look weak.
- **Enns move**: "Results are co-created — you have to do your part, we have to do ours. What I CAN guarantee: (a) we deliver everything in the SOW, (b) if the first deliverable doesn't meet spec, we redo it at no cost, (c) you can cancel month-to-month with 30 days notice."
- **For higher-trust clients**: performance-based pricing (% of tracked revenue) solves this elegantly — you're literally sharing the risk.

### 7. "We tried an agency before and it didn't work"
- **Decode**: trust wound. Don't be defensive about "other agencies."
- **NEPQ response**: "I hear that a lot. What specifically didn't work?"
- **Listen for**: (a) agency overpromised, (b) no reporting/visibility, (c) no strategy, only execution, (d) didn't understand their industry.
- **Reframe**: address each cause specifically in how you'll be different. Examples + proof, not claims.

### 8. "This isn't a priority right now"
- **What they mean**: the consequence cost isn't clear to them.
- **NEPQ consequence question**: "Okay — if it's not a priority, how are you currently handling [specific pain they mentioned on the call]?"
- **Then**: "In 6 months, if nothing changes, what does that look like?"
- **Let them paint the picture.** Most will realize the cost of inaction is higher than the cost of action.

### 9. "Can we start small?"
- **Good sign**: they want to buy, just risk-averse.
- **Response**: offer a pilot SOW — 30 days, fixed scope, fixed fee ($2K-$5K), credit toward full retainer if they continue.
- **Red flag**: "can we start with just the consult?" — that's a discovery-tax dodge. You already did discovery on the call. If they want more strategy without commitment, they're shopping strategy, not buying service.

### 10. "Can you do it for [50-70% of quoted price]?"
- **Enns iron rule**: discount = scope reduction, never price cut.
- **Response**: "I can't drop the price on that scope. I CAN reduce the scope — for example, we could do [module A] only for $X, and you'd add [module B] in Q3. Would that work?"
- **If they say no**: "Totally fair. The $X version of this isn't the right fit. When you're ready for the full scope, the door's open."
- **Walk away gracefully.** Discounting trains them that your price is negotiable and destroys the pricing integrity for your next 10 deals.

---

## Meta-principle: positioning defuses most objections upstream

Dunford: if your positioning is tight, 6 of these 10 objections never fire in the first place. "Too expensive" becomes "well, for an AI-automation specialist for home-services businesses, of course this costs more than a generalist VA."

If the same objection keeps firing, the objection isn't the problem. The positioning is.
